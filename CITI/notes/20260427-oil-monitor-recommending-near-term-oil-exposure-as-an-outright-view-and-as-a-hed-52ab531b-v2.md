## 核心观点 / Key Takeaways
1. 花旗将布伦特原油0-3个月目标价上调至120美元/桶（较当前价格约13%的上涨空间），并将2026年第二、三、四季度平均价格预测分别上调至110/95/80美元/桶（此前为95/80/75美元/桶）。

2. 报告建议投资者增加近期石油敞口，既可作为直接投资观点，也可作为对冲工具，以应对伊朗政权坚持其红线（至少持续到5月底）最大化"威慑、石油收入和复仇"(DOV)效用函数的策略。

3. 尽管市场对当前局势保持乐观，但花旗认为这是由于此前冲突前库存充足、国际能源署释放战略储备以及市场预期冲突会较快解决等因素所致，这些因素可能无法持续。

4. 即使石油流动恢复，商业和战略库存的重建需求意味着油价将在未来几年而非几个月内保持结构性强劲，这将为油价提供长期支撑。

## 关键数据 / Key Data
- 布伦特原油0-3个月目标价：120美元/桶（约13%上涨空间）
- 2026年季度平均价格预测（基础情景，50%概率）：第二季度110美元/桶，第三季度95美元/桶，第四季度80美元/桶
- 牛市情景（30%概率）：布伦特原油0-3个月价格150美元/桶，2026年季度平均价格130/130/100美元/桶
- 熊市情景（20%概率）：2026年季度平均价格95/80/75美元/桶
- 预计全球石油库存将在2026年7月底降至十年来的最低水平（若霍尔木兹海峡在5月底开放）
- 预计每日损失约1300万桶原油和产品产量

## 投资建议 / Investment Recommendation
买入/增持 (Buy/Increase)
花旗建议投资者增加近期石油敞口，既可作为直接投资观点，也可作为对冲工具。报告推荐受益于布伦特原油曲线前端约60-70%年化展期收益的策略，即使快速达成协议，展期收益也可能足以弥补任何现货损失，使对冲随时间推移自我抵消。

## 风险提示 / Risk Factors
1. 地缘政治风险：伊朗与美国谈判失败或未能取得实质性进展，导致霍尔木兹海峡持续关闭或受限时间超出预期。

2. 价格波动风险：若冲突持续或升级，油价可能飙升至150-180美元/桶的高位，对全球经济和金融市场造成冲击。

3. 库存消耗速度超预期：全球石油库存消耗速度可能快于预期，导致供应短缺加剧。

4. 需求破坏风险：高油价可能导致全球石油需求下降，尤其是如果经济受到严重影响。

5. 军事行动风险：美国可能采取军事行动，包括破坏伊朗能源基础设施，这将进一步扰乱全球石油供应。

Source: Citi Research, FGE, OilX Source: Citi Research, FGE, OilX Discussing the Iranian DOV utility function and US considerations which underpin our near term bullishness Our near-term bullish oil price thesis is grounded in the assumption that the Iranian regime is likely to be attempting to maximise a composite DOV utility function comprised of Deterrence, Oil revenue (future), and Vengeance. Deterrence against future external attacks - increases over time as global economic pain intensifies. Sustained high or rising energy and food prices impose incremental economic and political costs on the US and its allies, potentially increasing the reluctance to escalate further and thereby strengthening deterrence against future attacks on regime leadership and core economic assets. Oil revenue from higher future oil prices – this increases over time through rising oil prices which increases the present value of future oil sales revenues (especially to the degree production is not cut). A sustained price increase—for example, oil at $120/bbl versus $80/bbl—materially raises the present value of future oil and refined product exports, on the order of tens of billions of dollars annually. This is of course likely to be conditional on oil storage capacity to some degree - however here we estimate that with a small cut in oil production, the vast bulk of output can be maintained for at least a month, and most likely 2-3mths (not infinite, but enough to hold out until global inventories fall to all time recorded lows of the past 25 years). Vengeance captures the non-economic, human component of the regime’s potential objective function, and may be theoretically satiated over time as global economic pain intensifies for Iran’s adversaries and their allies. In an ideological system that has recently suffered losses among leadership, clerical figures, or senior cadres, there may be value attached to imposing visible economic pain on adversaries. Put simply, to maximise DOV utility, the regime would be incentivised to keep the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed or impaired, thereby tightening global oil supply, accelerating inventory drawdowns, and pushing oil prices materially higher. We do not expect this to last indefinitely, but we certainly expect it can last for at least another month (our base case of oil), if not through the end of June (our bull case for oil). Clearly, the Iranian regime’s maximization of the DOV utility function is conditional on a number of factors, in particular anything that threatens the regime’s survival. Thus their actions may be constrained by the desire to avoid triggering internal regime instability / civil war, and/or to avoid triggering an external response that threatens regime stability (either directly from external military actions or through pressure from its allies i.e. China does not want Iranian energy infra and regional ME infra destroyed). Higher oil prices, and lower US equity and bond prices are potential triggers for both President Trump to take military action (including energy infrastructure destruction), that could threaten the regimes survival, and thus this could be a trigger for Iran to deal in order to avoid that action. Indeed, the probability that the DOV maximizing strategy becomes unsustainable—and that the regime must pivot toward de-escalation or a negotiated outcome—rises along two dimensions: Time - as US and global political patience erodes the risk of military escalation rises, while also the likelihood of domestic unrest and problems with long term oil well output related to shut-ins increases (see below); and US Market Stress - equity and bond market sell-offs and higher inflation on the back of higher oil prices increase the political and economic pressure on US leadership, raising the likelihood of decisive military action once stress thresholds are breached. One potential constraint on how long this strategy can be sustained may be Iran’s ability to store unsold crude and products. We estimate that a complete oil export blockade scenario would see reported Iranian storage fill in about a month, but with some workarounds and modest output cuts, and/or utilizing additional hidden/underground storage, it would likely take at least 2–3 months to see major/material shut-ins. With exports stopped completely and production at 3.5 million barrels per day while domestic refineries used 2.1 million, the excess 1.4 million barrels per day would flow into storage; with 52 million barrels of empty space and ~90% usable (about 46.8 million barrels), that space would fill in roughly 33 days (46.8 ÷ 1.4). We believe that some oil would probably still be exported—about 350,000 barrels per day via Jask and ~200,000 by truck—cutting the net buildup to about 0.85 million barrels per day and extending the timeline to around 2 months. Allowing for modest production cuts and some additional hidden or underground storage would extend this out to 3 months or potentially even longer. On the US side, several factors reduce near‑term pressure to deal, however none of these are infinite, either. Factors include relatively low oil consumption intensity (please see the next section for charts of this), its dual mandate for monetary policy (which means it is less likely to raise rates in response to an inflation shock than pure inflation targeting countries), and the fact the US is a major producer as well as consumer (left pocket/right pocket offsets in terms of nominal GDP growth at least). Having said this, these factors are not infinite either. Eventually, the US administration may decide it is in its interest to enact material concessions in the face of substantially higher oil prices. Why are markets so sanguine? What does oil spending look like as a share of GDP in our various scenarios? Many clients are asking us why oil isn’t higher already, and why risk assets have been trading so well. Overall our answer is that oil stocks were ample coming into this shock, a US/Iran military or diplomatic resolution is generally expected (since the scale of the issue is so large), and on the pass through to growth side of things oil intensity is materially lower relative to GDP, especially in the US. On the oil side the answer is mainly that inventories built ~800mb over the 12 months leading up to the conflict, and that the IEA stock releases are helping to smooth out the shock. We have only got through over half of that (~500mb cumulative shut ins to date), and much of that has been satiated by strategic stock releases (which in total will end up being 400mb). Should there be a MOU/deal now and the Strait flows begin to recover over the coming week or so, inventories by the end of June would be low, but not extremely low, back to a touch below where they were a year ago.
