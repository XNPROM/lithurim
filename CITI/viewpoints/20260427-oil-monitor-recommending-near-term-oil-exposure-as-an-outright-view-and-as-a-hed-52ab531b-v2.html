CITI'S TAKE

Early last week we wrote about how we were ready to pivot to a more bullish price deck should the 2nd round of US/Iran negotiations fail or fail to materialise. In this note we do just that, delaying our SoH reopening base case to the end of May (from mid-late April), re-highlighting our bullish 0-3 month near term price target for Brent of $120/bbl (~13% upside) and raising our base case average price forecasts higher to $110/95/80/bbl for 2Q/3Q/4Q 2026, respectively (from $95/80/75/bbl), to which scenario we place a 50% indicative probability. We recommend near-dated oil exposure as an outright view and as a hedge against the regime maximizing a Deterrence, Oil revenue, and Vengeance (DOV) utility function by sticking to its red lines through the end of May (at least).

Our bull case scenario remains that flows could easily remain disrupted (to a similar degree to what we are seeing today) through the end of June (a relatively high 30% indicative probability) - which could see Brent reach $150/bbl, and prices average around $130/$130/$100 for 2Q/3Q/4Q 2026. We also discuss a super bull case oil scenario where the Strait is not opened by the end of June, and its implications for oil expenditure as a share of GDP both globally and in the US.

With both sides still far apart on their red lines we see the risks surrounding our bullish near term and our 2H’26 central case oil price forecasts as skewed to the upside, and continue to recommend clients hedge these risks with strategies that benefit from the roughly 60-70% annualized roll yield at the front of the Brent oil curve. The roll yield could well be so big as to repay any spot losses in the case of a quick deal, such that the hedge pays for itself over time.

The lack of extreme oil pricing in recent weeks, considering the scale of the loss of supply, is in our view mostly due to the large inventory build we had over the year preceding the conflict, which has provided a substantial buffer, alongside IEA stock releases, and an assumption that the conflict is too big not to be resolved relatively quickly. Combined with the so far relatively contained oil price increases with lower oil intensity, particularly in the US, and it’s possible to see why risk assets and risk exposed commodities like copper are holding up well so far.

Indeed, in this note we provide a detailed analysis of US and global crude oil and product expenditures as a share of GDP and find that what has happened to date, and what is implied by the declining oil forwards appears as something of a blip on the 55 year chart. Oil intensity is that much lower that we find that to see a rerun of the mid-2008 and late 1970s oil expenditure on oil products as a share of GDP, we would need to see all-in crude (i.e. product) prices of ~$280/bbl in the US (WTI likely well above $200/bbl), and $220/bbl for the world (Brent likely >$160-180/bbl).

For reference: A critical week - we estimate global oil inventories and prices under different forward paths and 2Q'26 Outlook - Energy to move higher, metals lower (for now), and how to trade and risk manage a 1970s type oil shock.

Higher for longer, with re-opening of Strait by end-May our new baseline

We are bullish Brent oil prices over the coming weeks and expect bullish front month rolling Brent oil strategies to continue to perform well owing to a likely sustained substantial backwardation, or roll yield (as one thing is for certain, crude and product stocks would be much lower coming out of this), both outright and as a hedge. We reiterate our 0-3 month Brent forecast at $120/bbl (~15% higher than current pricing), and raise our average 2Q/3Q/4Q forecasts to $110/$95/$80/bbl (50% indicative probability), alongside delaying our assumption of the opening of the SoH by one month to mid-end May from mid-end of this month. This means our baseline is the loss of 1.3bn barrels, up from 900mb, with only 500mb of that occurring to date, and 800mb of draws to come, taking stocks down to their lowest level in over a decade in days of consumption. Our up-to-date base case cross-commodity price can always be found here.

Critically, even once flows resume, the need to rebuild both commercial and strategic inventories implies structurally stronger prices for years rather than months.

Our bull scenario (30%) remains 0-3 month price of $150/bbl and $130/$130/$100/bbl for 2Q/3Q/4Q for 2026 (we have 100 average for 2027 in the bull case scenario), which is based on two months of losses at the recent run rates, or 1.7bn barrels total losses. There is of course a super bullish scenario which involves mutual energy infrastructure destruction and/or ongoing losses beyond 2 months, where prices trade up to or higher than $160-180/bbl Brent for a sustained period, consistent with sustained >$200/bbl all-in prices.

Our original thesis was that this situation was simply too large to remain unresolved, and while this remains the case, the stress so far has not been significant enough, and so more time and higher oil prices appear likely to be required to see a MOU leading to deal (please see our section on Iranian regime and US considerations). As such, our old base case scenario (involving an imminent deal) becomes the new bear case scenario ($95/$80/$75/bbl for 2Q/3Q/4Q), at a 20% indicative probability. Indeed, both sides seem willing to engage, but largely on their own terms. We continue to expect that the stress imposed on the system (including lower equity and/or bond prices) can ultimately force some combination of diplomatic and/or military actions, leading to a resolution that allowed oil flows to begin recovering by mid‑ to late May in our updated base case, and mid-June in our new bull case scenario.

We are set to see crude and product inventories globally reach their lowest levels in 8 years by the end of June (this is locked in already) and their lowest levels in over a decade by the end of July should the opening of the Strait be delayed until the end of May – our new base case (please see Figure 1), which illustrates the anticipated draws with the dotted dark blue line, with the light blue line global consumption weighted oil product prices including diesel and gasoline. We estimate that we have lost around 500mb of crude oil and products output since the beginning of the conflict (see Figure 3). This loss figure will almost triple if the Strait does not open until the end of May, with a further 800bn barrels of estimated production losses by the end of July, taking into account the time it may take for mines to be swept, logistical constraints to be resolved (including shipping issues and crude oil and refinery output ramp up times etc), and energy infrastructure to be repaired. Total losses would be ~1.3bn barrels.

One thing is certain is that oil inventories by 3Q’26 would be around their lowest levels seen over the past decade, or lower (please see Figure 3). We expect this will support a solid backwardation through the remaining of this year, with any oil surplus likely absorbed by strategic stockpile replenishment should there be an eventual deal and ramp up of ME capacity (including previous spare capacity).

Rebuilding stocks will support crude prices for years to come. Of the 900mb of ‘locked-in’ losses and 1.3bn barrel projected losses, around 400mb of strategic stocks will one day need to be rebuilt (that’s ~1mb/d for over a year), and many countries may wish to build new stockpiles, particularly of Middle Eastern grade crude oil (typically high middle distillate yielding grades – diesel and jet fuel) in crude, as well as stocks of diesel and jet fuel. This will be especially true if the truce appears fragile.

Discussing the Iranian DOV utility function and US considerations which underpin our near term bullishness

Our near-term bullish oil price thesis is grounded in the assumption that the Iranian regime is likely to be attempting to maximise a composite DOV utility function comprised of Deterrence, Oil revenue (future), and Vengeance.

  • Deterrence against future external attacks - increases over time as global economic pain intensifies. Sustained high or rising energy and food prices impose incremental economic and political costs on the US and its allies, potentially increasing the reluctance to escalate further and thereby strengthening deterrence against future attacks on regime leadership and core economic assets.

  • Oil revenue from higher future oil prices – this increases over time through rising oil prices which increases the present value of future oil sales revenues (especially to the degree production is not cut). A sustained price increase—for example, oil at $120/bbl versus $80/bbl—materially raises the present value of future oil and refined product exports, on the order of tens of billions of dollars annually. This is of course likely to be conditional on oil storage capacity to some degree - however here we estimate that with a small cut in oil production, the vast bulk of output can be maintained for at least a month, and most likely 2-3mths (not infinite, but enough to hold out until global inventories fall to all time recorded lows of the past 25 years).

  • Vengeance captures the non-economic, human component of the regime’s potential objective function, and may be theoretically satiated over time as global economic pain intensifies for Iran’s adversaries and their allies. In an ideological system that has recently suffered losses among leadership, clerical figures, or senior cadres, there may be value attached to imposing visible economic pain on adversaries.

Put simply, to maximise DOV utility, the regime would be incentivised to keep the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed or impaired, thereby tightening global oil supply, accelerating inventory drawdowns, and pushing oil prices materially higher. We do not expect this to last indefinitely, but we certainly expect it can last for at least another month (our base case of oil), if not through the end of June (our bull case for oil).

Clearly, the Iranian regime’s maximization of the DOV utility function is conditional on a number of factors, in particular anything that threatens the regime’s survival. Thus their actions may be constrained by the desire to avoid triggering internal regime instability / civil war, and/or to avoid triggering an external response that threatens regime stability (either directly from external military actions or through pressure from its allies i.e. China does not want Iranian energy infra and regional ME infra destroyed).

Higher oil prices, and lower US equity and bond prices are potential triggers for both President Trump to take military action (including energy infrastructure destruction), that could threaten the regimes survival, and thus this could be a trigger for Iran to deal in order to avoid that action. Indeed, the probability that the DOV maximizing strategy becomes unsustainable—and that the regime must pivot toward de-escalation or a negotiated outcome—rises along two dimensions:

  • Time - as US and global political patience erodes the risk of military escalation rises, while also the likelihood of domestic unrest and problems with long term oil well output related to shut-ins increases (see below); and

  • US Market Stress - equity and bond market sell-offs and higher inflation on the back of higher oil prices increase the political and economic pressure on US leadership, raising the likelihood of decisive military action once stress thresholds are breached.

One potential constraint on how long this strategy can be sustained may be Iran’s ability to store unsold crude and products. We estimate that a complete oil export blockade scenario would see reported Iranian storage fill in about a month, but with some workarounds and modest output cuts, and/or utilizing additional hidden/underground storage, it would likely take at least 2–3 months to see major/material shut-ins. With exports stopped completely and production at 3.5 million barrels per day while domestic refineries used 2.1 million, the excess 1.4 million barrels per day would flow into storage; with 52 million barrels of empty space and ~90% usable (about 46.8 million barrels), that space would fill in roughly 33 days (46.8 ÷ 1.4). We believe that some oil would probably still be exported—about 350,000 barrels per day via Jask and ~200,000 by truck—cutting the net buildup to about 0.85 million barrels per day and extending the timeline to around 2 months. Allowing for modest production cuts and some additional hidden or underground storage would extend this out to 3 months or potentially even longer.

On the US side, several factors reduce near‑term pressure to deal, however none of these are infinite, either. Factors include relatively low oil consumption intensity (please see the next section for charts of this), its dual mandate for monetary policy (which means it is less likely to raise rates in response to an inflation shock than pure inflation targeting countries), and the fact the US is a major producer as well as consumer (left pocket/right pocket offsets in terms of nominal GDP growth at least). Having said this, these factors are not infinite either. Eventually, the US administration may decide it is in its interest to enact material concessions in the face of substantially higher oil prices.

Why are markets so sanguine? What does oil spending look like as a share of GDP in our various scenarios?

Many clients are asking us why oil isn’t higher already, and why risk assets have been trading so well. Overall our answer is that oil stocks were ample coming into this shock, a US/Iran military or diplomatic resolution is generally expected (since the scale of the issue is so large), and on the pass through to growth side of things oil intensity is materially lower relative to GDP, especially in the US.

On the oil side the answer is mainly that inventories built ~800mb over the 12 months leading up to the conflict, and that the IEA stock releases are helping to smooth out the shock. We have only got through over half of that (~500mb cumulative shut ins to date), and much of that has been satiated by strategic stock releases (which in total will end up being 400mb). Should there be a MOU/deal now and the Strait flows begin to recover over the coming week or so, inventories by the end of June would be low, but not extremely low, back to a touch below where they were a year ago. However, should our base case come true we would draw to levels well below last year, and we can see higher prices than today in the coming weeks.

On the broader risk-asset side of things the relatively sanguine trading environment we have seen is at least partly to do with oil intensity being lower today than it was in the past (on top of the fact the price reaction hasn’t been overly extreme, and that the markets seem to expect the situation to resolve fairly quickly from here). This can be seen in the work in the next 6 charts - Figures 13 through 18. In Figures 13 and 15 we show the value of oil product consumption globally, in nominal terms. The rise looks very big - in the vicinity of $2tr annualized increase in costs ytd – however, this is only 1.6% of GDP, and it looks like a modest blip if one assumes a deal is done relatively quickly and oil prices come back down over the next 12 months. This is even more true looking at the US charts, where oil started as a lower share of GDP, and rose to a lower share of GDP than that of the world.
