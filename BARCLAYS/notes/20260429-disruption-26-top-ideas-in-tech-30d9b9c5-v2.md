## 核心观点 / Key Takeaways
1. 巴克莱对科技板块持选择性支持态度，认为科技股有上涨空间。对冲基金增加了对纳斯达克指数(NDX)的对冲，而主动型共同基金(LO)正在将现金重新配置到半导体而非软件领域。
Barclays is selectively supportive of Tech upside from here. Uptick in NDX beta hedging by fast money; long-only (LO) managers are redeploying cash and rotating to Semis from Software.

2. 互联网领域：大型科技股与其它公司分化。随着AI采用增加，大型科技股在堆栈的多个层面参与；巴克莱互联网团队表示会在第一季度财报前增持仓位。
Internet: Mega caps vs. everything else. As AI adoption increases, mega caps participate across several layers of the stack; our Internet team states they would add to positions into 1Q earnings.

3. 半导体领域：市场"不公平地定价了资本支出峰值"。大型AI芯片公司估值处于低位，而衍生品名称交易接近峰值。
Semis & Semi-Cap: Unfairly pricing "peak capex." Our Semiconductor team argues large AI chip names are trading at valuation lows while derivative names are trading near peak.

## 风险提示 / Risk Factors
1. AI对不同商业模式的颠覆程度存在不确定性，可能影响非大型科技股的表现。
2. 宏观经济不确定性可能导致投资者情绪波动，影响科技股表现。
3. 零售投资者对科技股反弹持怀疑态度，可能限制上涨空间。
4. 大型科技股面临地缘政治、司法和宏观风险。
5. 部分公司可能因AI效率而进行裁员，影响市场情绪。

U.S. Equity Strategy Venu Krishna, CFA BCI, US | Rex Feng BCI, US Back in January, we wrote about the S&P 500 delivering its 7th best 3-year run since 1930, with TMT accounting for more than half of the index's total return in 2023, 2024 and 2025. As a fragile ceasefire in the Middle East and the arrival of earnings season turn investor focus back to growth drivers, large-cap Tech stocks seem to be looking for a repeat performance: Semis, Media and Hardware powered more than half of SPX total return since the March 30 low. However, as the AI narrative shifts from "rising tide lifts all boats" to winners and losers (more detail on that here), participation in the latest Tech rally is not as uniform as it has been in the past. From a positioning standpoint, we are seeing material shifts in who the marginal Tech buyer is and how that buyer responds to valuation, volatility, and macro uncertainty. Long-only managers and hedge funds are more selective, flows have become more discriminating, and retail investors have turned more skeptical. Long-only funds are adding selectively in Tech Entering 2026, active generalist funds were unwinding what had been a historically outsized underweight in Tech, going from a near-6% underweight as of C3Q-end to a more reasonable (4.4%), the smallest underweight in 3 years. Within Tech, Semis and Software retained mid-cycle allocations, while Internet and Hardware sat closer to the bottom of historical weight ranges; consistent with active managers rotating toward perceived “picks-and-shovels” beneficiaries of AI rather than duration-heavy growth franchises. Higher frequency data leads us to believe that the generalist cohort has continued to add back Tech exposure YTD, although with nuance at the industry level. Re-risking has been driven almost entirely by Semis, while Software exposure has continued to lag. This aligns with what the Software team has been hearing on the road: underweight Software in favor of "safer" themes like Semis (Raimo's Roundup: Investor Feedback From Our Extensive Marketing, 17 Apr 2026). In that context, the Tech re-weighting looks less like a bullish sector call and more like a narrowing of exposure to segments with clearer near-term cash flow visibility and tangible AI leverage. The flow-through of LO generalist demand to price action was likely muted until very recently, as PMs had reduced effective equity risk early in 1Q via higher cash balances. Even where managers were less structurally bearish on Tech, incremental dollars were not being redeployed aggressively, generally blunting the mechanical impact of reduced underweights. Growth portfolios in particular raised cash sharply amid rising macro uncertainty. However, the latest data shows that active managers were on their way back toward fully invested in March, which likely extended into April as sentiment switched back to risk-on. Hedge funds hunt for idiosyncratic upside By late February, hedge fund equity exposure had reached cycle highs, concurrent with Tech leadership being reinforced by momentum, factor alignment, and index effects. However, as performance momentum faded and macro risks re-emerged in March, de-grossing followed quickly. Nevertheless, we don't think hedge funds exited Tech as swiftly as they did the rest of the market, perhaps guided by very recent memories of how quickly the market can turn in response to capricious US policy. Since late February, the divergence between leveraged fund shorting of NDX futures and rising dealer absorption suggests active index-level beta management by hedge funds, which typically coincides with maintaining underlying directional or idiosyncratic exposures rather than wholesale de-risking. In line with that, exposure within net long equity hedged growth portfolios was not reduced as aggressively as exposure within value or macro portfolios. Of course, absent visibility into single-name holdings, we cannot definitively distinguish between outright macro shorts and beta overlay hedging, but the asymmetric risk transfer to dealers and timing relative to overall equity market dynamics argues for the latter. On a related note, single-stock options activity surged nearly $200Bn over the 2 weeks ending April 16th, the 2nd largest ever, led by ORCL, other AI-related names and mega-caps. Rising call buy/sell-to-open ratio signals upside chasing, with institutions capturing a larger share of call option activity (The Long & Short of It: Macro fear gives way to micro FOMO, 21 Apr 2026). Are anticipated IPOs causing investors to reserve capital? We've considered whether speculation around upcoming large Tech IPOs (including OpenAI and Anthropic in the 2026-2027 time frame as reported in the Wall Street Journal, plus other PE- and VC-backed unicorns that amount to trillions of dollars of value looking for an exit) is affecting PM allocations to Tech, particularly in the highly liquid large/mega-cap segment. We doubt that this is a major factor. IPO waves typically correspond with strong markets, when healthy capital flows should readily facilitate new issue absorption, even for large deals in which the total raise is a minor fraction of firm value. Further, an event study of prior Tech IPO waves fails to establish a strong linkage between the "expectation" phase of IPO ramp-ups and Tech vs. SPX relative returns. CTAs likely to keep adding to US & US Tech equities near term Across systematic strategies, equity exposure remains low, but we find the potential for further inflows differ materially. CTAs moved quickly to buy the dip in US equities after the US-Iran ceasefire eased tail risks, and we think are likely to keep adding to US equities in the near-term (The Long & Short of It: Macro fear gives way to micro FOMO, 21 Apr 2026). We observe a similar pattern for US Tech equities, where CTA positioning points to continued buying of US equities across most scenarios. Fund flows have become more price conscious After a prolonged period of mostly steady inflows over the 2 years ending April 2025, new money coming into dedicated Tech long-only funds became much more reactive to valuations over the last year, as correlations between Tech fund flows and changes in NTM P/E turned negative. In practical terms, inflows increasingly showed up during drawdowns rather than during multiple expansion, suggesting that Tech-specialist investors transitioned from chasing upside to opportunistic dip-buying. This counter-cyclical posturing alters the marginal impact of flows on prices, with reactive demand acting as a stabilizer rather than an upside driver. This dynamic helps explain why Tech relative returns decelerated in 4Q25 and 1Q26; with specialist fund flows no longer validating higher multiples, Tech appeared less of a must-own sector for the end-user investor, consistent with a market more disciplined on valuation. However, this behavior has once again switched gears over recent weeks, and valuation-flow correlations heading back toward neutral could signal a long-only investor base more willing to join the chase for upside, in our view. "Left on read" by retail investors Direct retail participation in equities has quietly but steadily faded since its mid‑2025 peak. Payment-for-order-flow data from the major retail brokers suggest that 2Q25 marked a historical high for small investor engagement, coinciding with some of the strongest Tech momentum of the last year. This has since trended lower despite episodic volatility spikes. Retail dip-buying provided incremental demand during Tech pullbacks throughout much of the current bull market. Over the last couple of quarters, that stabilizing force appears weaker. If anything, we believe recent flows into and out of leveraged long and short QQQ ETFs suggest that retail investors are keen to fade the current Tech rebound. The ratio of flows favors short over long by the widest margin since May 2025, which at the time was also a rapid Tech-led recovery from macro disruption ("Liberation Day"). Concurrently, retail investors are also now commanding the largest share of overall put option activity since May 2022 (The Long & Short of It: Macro fear gives way to micro FOMO, 21 Apr 2026). Putting the pieces together All in, we think positioning looks supportive of further Tech upside potential, with selectivity at the industry and single-stock level. Hedge funds continue to pursue idiosyncratic opportunities. Long-only managers on the whole are redeploying cash with a preference for Semis over Software; in our view, allocations have room to run in both directions based on historical ranges. Fund flows remain modestly price conscious in the Tech space but much less so than a few months ago, and could be poised to flip to upside chasing, particularly as sectoral flow trends from 1Q26 (Energy, Industrials, etc.) begin to weaken. Retail investors appear doubtful of the current rebound, but we think solid 1Q26 results could motivate some bullish capitulation, potentially bringing back an additional channel of demand for equities that has taken a step back over recent quarters. Outside of Mega Cap: AI Winners vs. AI Laggards The main bull-bear debate for the rest of our coverage, excluding the mega caps, has been how disruptive will AI be to different business models. In our recent deep dive, Framing AI Defensibility Across Online Marketplace Models, we provide a framework to help investors gauge the degree to which different characteristics of various marketplace businesses could be disrupted by AI. The main factors denoting how defensible a marketplace would be to potential AI disruption, in our view, are marketplace structure, price point/frequency of purchases, and the replicability of supply and demand. On the first point, we found that managed/heavily managed marketplaces, which include features like inventory custody, fulfillment, returns, facilitating transactions with contracted drivers/couriers (in the case of gig economy), etc., screen as the most defensible to AI disruption. Lead gen/pure transactional marketplaces screen as being less defensible to AI disruption, in our view. The deeper the marketplace is involved in the transaction and selection, the "stickier" we think the platform is, and thus more difficult to disintermediate. On purchase frequency/price point, we see higher price/lower frequency purchases as more susceptible to disintermediation. For these purchases, consumers are generally less habituated to one platform, and higher price point typically necessitates greater comparison – something we think AI can do well, which could provide a foothold to provide further value. The offset here to these high value, infrequent transactions is that the trust factor is much higher; AI making a mistake booking a vacation is much more problematic than an error on a food delivery order. Supply and demand's replicability varies depending on product type and scale, but we see unique supply particularly as a differentiator that is more difficult for AI to replicate. We encourage investors to use our framework to help analyze business models across consumer internet. AI Finally Impacting Headcount? U.S. Software: Large-Cap, Infrastructure, & Horizontal SaaS Raimo Lenschow, CFA BCI, US | Eamon Coughlin BCI, US | Kartikeya Atreya Kotamraju BCI, US | Becky Sun BCI, US Key Debates Is AI the Next Big Thing or a Threat to Enterprise Application Software Vendors? The first key debate is whether AI is a major growth opportunity for enterprise software vendors or if AI will prove to be a structural risk for (some) enterprise software vendors. In our view, we see AI as a compelling opportunity for many names in our coverage. However, companies will not see equal returns from AI monetization. Broadly, there are three aspects of the debate around AI being a potential threat to application software vendors that most frequently come up in conversations on the subject. Why We View Enterprise Vendors as More Insulated and Better Positioned to Monetize AI: In October 2025 and March 2026, we hosted two calls with the Barclays CTO who expressed the belief that AI companies are not replacing SaaS applications, but driving greater ease of use and productivity. Ultimately, the Barclays CTO noted that enterprises should strive for a balanced approach to AI; leveraging both broad and niche AI capabilities, ensuring foundational guardrails are in place, and viewing AI as an additive force rather than a wholesale replacement for existing systems. Below we outline the key underlying reasons why we believe SaaS vendors will be the vector with which AI value accrues over time: Data gravity and metadata advantages: Deep integration of enterprise data and metadata creates defensible moats. See more on this topic: rAImo's AI Series - Vol. 2: The Data Gold Rush; 9/11/2023. Embedded workflows and automations: Mission-critical processes are deeply tied to existing platforms. Integration ecosystems: Strong connectivity across enterprise systems via out of the box connectors and marketplaces limit switching. Deployment flexibility: Options for on-prem, hybrid, and cloud deployments meet diverse customer and workload needs. Security and compliance standards: Enterprise-grade security remains a differentiator for incumbents, with certain certifications (FedRamp) taking time. Distribution channels / professional services: Most enterprise SaaS companies have an ecosystem of partners that support the implementation, education, and ongoing utility of tools that make adoption more seamless. Are We in an AI Infrastructure Bubble? The second AI debate that we think will significantly influence software investing in 2026 is whether or not the market is in an "AI Bubble," and the implications. Constructive views on this subject point to the unprecedented growth and market opportunity around newer AI advancements, with more negative views fixating on potential customer concentration and financing risks that lead to vendors overbuilding for demand that will not materialize, eventually creating credit event risks from the significant debt being taken on to deploy AI capacity. In our view, it will be difficult for CY26 to answer all of the debates around the AI industry as there are simply structural aspects, such as lack of visibility into the costs, energy requirements, etc. of future GPU generations, that are impossible to overcome. However, in our view, the market is overly fixating on single customer risks and not giving appropriate credit to the large, diverse opportunity ahead for AI infrastructure providers. In particular, we see demand as broader than what recent performance for the space implies, and we see more options around financing and deployment types that could help reduce financing/credit rating downgrade concerns. Put together, we think this creates a compelling entry point for Microsoft, Oracle, and DigitalOcean in CY26. AI infrastructure demand is more broad than just model makers and hyperscalers: We believe we are in the very early innings of enterprise and consumer AI adoption and expect usage to grow exponentially in the coming years. Currently, a significant share of the AI demand is from select AI labs and hyperscalers. However, in the future, we expect this to fall to broader enterprises, organizations and consumers. We believe much of the current concerns affecting recent performance stems around the near-term outlook for specific AI labs, with less focus on the larger, long-term enterprise opportunity. We do not find it difficult to underwrite AI usage to see exponential growth in the coming years as AI advancements allow for more machine to machine interactions that drive business processes (e.g., AI agent proliferation, discussed further below). In addition, we believe the market is underappreciating future AI demand from governments and agencies to drive greater efficiency in processes, research better solutions to challenges faced and constituent outcomes. U.S. Software: Security, Design, and Vertical SaaS Saket Kalia, CFA BCI, US | Ryan Powderly-Gross BCI, US | Carly Buecker BCI, US | Alyssa Lee BCI, US | Ethan Sneckenberger BCI, US | Joseph Pilleteri BCI, US Key debates in our security, design, and vertical SaaS coverage Could new tools created by AI vendors as well as enabling enterprises to vibe code their own solutions using low-code AI tools ultimately displace incumbent SaaS and security vendors? The most topical debate in our coverage and the primary reason we think software has underperformed YTD (IGV down 19% versus SPX up 5% YTD) is investors have questioned whether tools created by AI vendors or the use of low-code tools from AI vendors like Claude, Cursor, and Gemini, which enable organizations to vibe code their own tools, create risk to terminal values in software. While AI vendors like Anthropic (private, not covered) and OpenAI (private, not covered) have introduced products in areas like code security and some IT buyers may be looking to replace certain point solution tools with their own custom, vibe-coded solutions, we are skeptical that this could realistically endanger all segments of software equally. As a result, we believe the software drawdown and the seemingly "guilty until proven innocent" market reaction is overdone, and we think security and vertical SaaS could present opportunities. In security, the criticality of cybersecurity creates a preference for security specialists over horizontal vendors, which is the reason horizontal platforms have had mixed success in security historically. Additionally, security buyers may be hesitant to rely on the same vendors that create AI models and tools to protect those tools as well. Historically, security buyers have opted for third-party security specialists to secure their cloud environments instead of using native tools from public cloud vendors like Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, all of which offer cloud security and cloud network security tools. Similarly, we think security teams may gravitate towards third-party security specialists over the security solutions offered by AI vendors. We also note that several security vendors including Palo Alto Networks and CrowdStrike are working with AI vendors like Anthropic and OpenAI to secure systems before more advanced frontier models become broadly available (like Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview and Project Glasswing, for example), which suggests that Anthropic and OpenAI are friends, not foes, to cybersecurity. We think this creates an opportunity to own major platform names with FCF support and growing AI revenue like PANW, CRWD, and ZS. In vertical SaaS, several of our coverage companies operate as “systems of record” for their customers’ businesses – they are purpose-built systems for insurance companies, governments, or pharma companies that have built up years of proprietary data that underpins daily operations for their customers. Therefore, we think these businesses are more defensive and harder to vibe-code (VEEV's R&D tools for pharma companies, TYL's tools for local and regional governments, and ADSK's design tools for architects and engineers, for example). In addition, some end-markets are particularly conservative IT buyers, like governments, pharma companies, and financial services companies, which we think would have the lowest probability to experiment with vibe-coded alternatives. In our view, this creates an opportunity to own systems of record with domain-specific data moats and conservative end markets like VEEV, TYL, and ADSK. Which end markets or vendors within software could see real AI benefits materialize in results? As we think about the relative positioning within software, we think the cybersecurity space is best positioned to see increased spending prioritization as AI expands the attack surface and can lead to a greater volume and sophistication of cyber attacks, and we believe the growing use of AI tools and agents in the enterprise creates a near-term revenue opportunity for security vendors with AI security offerings. We've talked to several security buyers and partners who are first prioritizing data security and identity security to ensure sensitive data is not accidentally leaked through prompts in AI models, control what data AI tools and agents have access to, and manage the identities and entitlements of agents. For endpoint security, as more AI agents, browsers and applications run on the endpoint, this positions the endpoint as a more strategic control point rather than just an enforcement layer. From a network security perspective, we wonder if growing AI and agentic traffic could lead to greater investments in network security architectures over time, and whether the build out of AI infrastructure could drive demand for firewalls in the near-term. For network infrastructure, as more agents are created, these agents consume content and make requests at a higher rate than humans. For CDN names like NET, this increase in traffic on their network is monetizable and needs to be secured, which should create a tailwind for both performance and security revenue as agent adoption increases. Lastly, we note several security vendors have released specific AI security solutions as well, which should create a discrete revenue opportunity both in the near- and long-term. Putting this all together, we think market leaders in data security and identity like VRNS, OKTA, and SAIL are well positioned to benefit from greater spending prioritization in those markets, and platform vendors with AI security offerings like PANW and CRWD are well positioned as enterprises will likely turn to trusted platform vendors to secure their AI estate, in our view. Will fundamentals for software names matter in the face of continued headline risk as leading AI vendors like Anthropic and OpenAI release new and enhanced tools/models? While we think several security and vertical SaaS vendors are positioned well in the face of potential AI disruption (like CRWD, PANW, and ZS in security and VEEV, TYL, and ADSK in vertical SaaS) and think fundamentals for many names in our coverage remain healthy, the question we get is whether risk from continued headlines from Anthropic and OpenAI releasing new models, enhanced capabilities, and security-focused tools will continue to weigh on software multiples despite the underlying fundamentals. We think this is a fair pushback, and investors will likely need to see the AI tailwinds we discuss above materialize in results for our coverage companies before the bear case around AI disruption in security and certain parts of vertical SaaS is disproved. Potential Near-Term Catalysts AI changes IT environments and makes hackers more productive, which could make security a higher spending priority. As enterprises accelerate AI adoption, IT environments are becoming more complex. At the same time, AI lowers the friction required to carry out attacks, allowing threat actors to operate more efficiently at scale. Putting this together, this dynamic increases the frequency and sophistication of cyberattacks, reinforcing security as a priority spend as AI becomes more embedded in core environments. Additionally, the changing IT environments drive an evolution of networks and workflows which could create new areas of cybersecurity. In this context, security increasingly acts as an enabler of enterprises rolling out AI initiatives. We could start to see AI materialize in the results for security vendors that have released AI security tools. Several security vendors in our coverage have introduced AI security tools both to secure AI usage and to improve productivity within the SOC, which we believe could eventually translate to incremental top-line contribution over time. Examples include Palo Alto's Prisma AIRS, CrowdStrike's Falcon AIDR and CharlotteAI, Okta for AI Agents, Cloudflare's AI Security Suite, Zscaler's AI Security. While it's still early for many of these solutions, some have been showing notable traction and we would expect contributions to materialize as deployments scale, potentially becoming more meaningful later this year and into next. What is the longevity of electrical connections for scale-up architectures and the timing of volume scale-up optics across the ecosystem? One of the most topical debates in our coverage universe is copper vs. optical, as data rates increase and bandwidth demands push the network ecosystem to its limits. Copper, long assumed to be nearing its physical limits, has proven more resilient than the bear case suggests with AVGO even suggesting the potential to do 800G on copper by 2028, which means scale-up on copper. CRDO’s AEC solutions have meaningfully extended copper's reach at 100G and 200G per-lane speeds. Copper remains significantly cheaper to deploy, and reliability is superior to optics. Our View on Memory Price Impacts We see very little scope for demand destruction (in dollar terms, not units) and believe that GM will be largely unaffected over the mid and longer term as a result of the higher DRAM and NAND prices. Our view is that enterprise spend decisions are made on a dollar basis as the company spends at least the amount that it allocates. Additionally, we believe there are a few ways that companies manage margins in times of inflation including 1) using inventory on hand or strategic pre-buys, 2) raising prices to account for commodity increases and 3) changing specs (e.g. more disc in a hybrid array vs AFA, less DRAM content in a server). The price increases by the OEMs tend to keep the GM % consistent over time. For example, if we assume NAND started at 40% of COGS, and prices increase by 90% this year, the offered ASP for the array needs to increase by 36%. In terms of memory content by product category, NAND can represent 30-40% of the BoM in an all flash array, and we assume DRAM is about 50% of BoM for servers in normal times, while networking doesn’t have the same intensity.
